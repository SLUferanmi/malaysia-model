Operational Workflow & Prediction Mechanics

How the model utilizes agricultural inputs to estimate crop yield in real-time

1. OLS Parameter Optimization

During the model training phase, the Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) algorithm analyzed 40 years of DOSM historical data to compute the coefficients that minimize the sum of squared prediction errors (residuals), establishing the mathematically optimized regression equation.

2. Live Slider Inference

When you adjust the interactive dashboard sliders, the system captures the values for Planted Area (x₁) and Rice Production (x₂) and feeds them directly into the regression equation to compute the estimated crop yield (ŷ) in real-time.

3. Coefficient Interpretation

The negative coefficient for Planted Area (-0.004403) indicates a dilution effect, where expanding land without matching output reduces average yield density. The positive coefficient for Rice Production (+0.002291) shows that increased output on stable land drives agricultural efficiency.

Model Specification & Formulation

Mathematical framework of the Multiple Linear Regression model

The Paddy Crop Yield Predictor uses an Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) Multiple Linear Regression model to model the linear relationship between agricultural inputs and average crop yield:

OLS Regression Equation
yᵢ = β₀ + β₁xᵢ₁ + β₂xᵢ₂ + εᵢ
Matrix Form Representation
Y = Xβ + ε
Normal Equation & Parameter Estimation
β̂ = (XᵀX)⁻¹XᵀY

Where Y is the n × 1 vector of observed average paddy yields, X is the n × 3 design matrix containing a column of ones for the intercept and the predictor columns, β̂ is the 3 × 1 vector of estimated coefficients, and ε represents the random error vector, assumed to be independent and identically distributed as N(0, σ²).

Dataset & Variable Specifications

Variables compiled from Department of Statistics Malaysia (1980–2019)

Diagnostic Analysis of OLS Assumptions

Empirical validation and checks performed on the residual errors and variables

Linearity of Relationship — Met

Visually verified via scatter plots and regression trend lines. The target variable (Average Yield) exhibits a near-perfect linear correlation with Rice Production (r = 0.9907) and a weak positive relationship with Planted Area (r = 0.2316), confirming that a linear model structure is appropriate for the data.

Absence of Multicollinearity — Met

Checked using the Variance Inflation Factor (VIF) on the regression design matrix. The centered VIF for both predictors (Planted Area and Rice Production) calculates to exactly 1.1478. Since this is well below the standard threshold of 5.0 (and conservative threshold of 10.0), multicollinearity is verified to be absent, proving both predictors add independent value.

Homoscedasticity (Constant Error Variance) — Met

Assessed via residuals-vs-fitted scatter plots. The residual values are distributed randomly and relatively uniformly around the zero line across the range of fitted values. No systematic patterns, such as a funnel shape (indicating heteroscedasticity), are observed, verifying stable error variance.

Residual Normality — Minor Time-Series Departure

The Shapiro-Wilk normality test on the OLS residuals yields a statistic of W = 0.7304 (p-value < 0.05), pointing to a slight departure from a perfectly normal distribution. This is a common characteristic of time-series crop datasets. In accordance with the Central Limit Theorem (n = 40), the OLS estimators remain unbiased, and the extremely high goodness of fit (R² = 99.92%) maintains strong predictive validity.
